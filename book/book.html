Preparation

Before starting formal meditation practice, it’s essential to spend some time establishing conditions that support stability and continuity of attention. When you build a house, the stronger the foundation, the more likely it is to withstand a storm. In the same way, we build a strong foundation for each meditation session so that it is not easily toppled.

Preparation Step 1 – Posture

We start by setting our posture. A good posture for meditation balances alertness and uprightness with ease and relaxation. Some muscular effort is always needed to keep the body aligned, but we aim to use as little muscle energy as needed to maintain balance without becoming rigid. Part of avoiding rigidity is recognizing that the body is never truly still - it is always in subtle motion, and this motion is tied to the breathing process. Seeing this clearly helps us notice the body’s natural movement rather than resist it. The goal is not perfect posture but a position that lets the breath move naturally and awareness remain steady.

Preparation Step 2 – Loving Kindness Meditation

There are numerous methods for loving kindness meditation, and every teacher interprets these methods differently. The specific method matters less than including two categories of beings in your well-wishes: (1) yourself, and (2) all beings everywhere. See Appendix REF for a description of some loving kindness methods you could use here.

This phase does not need to be lengthy - a couple of minutes can suffice. You may silently repeat a phrase, such as “May I be safe, happy and well” a few times, followed by repeating “May all beings everywhere be safe, happy and well.” Whatever method you use, try to generate the feeling of the brahmavihara you’re practicing. Loving kindness practice softens the heart, helping to reduce hindrances like aversion and restlessness.

Preparation Step 3 – Motivation

The reasons for pursuing formal meditation practice are as numerous as its benefits - spiritual, mental health related, or otherwise - and every person has their own. Whatever yours are, bring them to mind by silently asking yourself, “Why am I doing this?” This helps reduce the hindrance of doubt, gives the mind clearer direction from the start, and is encouraging.

Preparation Step 4 – Session Plan

Briefly go over the plan for this formal meditation session. Check in and ask yourself how your mind is doing: is it exhilarated, agitated, relaxed, contracted, exalted, or something else? Here you can tweak the plan to fit your state of mind. It’s useful to pre-plan any changes to your usual routine, so fewer decisions have to be made during the meditation itself. For example, your plan might specify how long to spend on particular steps, or it may include a practice variation you’re trying out.

Preparation Step 5 – Present Moment Awareness

Abandon the past. Abandon the future. Shift your attention to the present moment. If you need a tool to help with this, you can try cycling between (a) telling yourself “I am aware that,” then (b) paying attention to whatever arises - thought, touch, sound, odor, taste, or sight - and noticing how your awareness of it rises and falls.

Ānāpānassati Instructions

Common Guidelines

  • Ideally, breathe both in and out through the nose. If that’s not possible or easy, breathe in through the nose and out through the mouth. As a last resort, breathe both in and out through the mouth.
  • If thoughts arise, and you notice them, congratulate yourself for having noticed them and for having reconnected with your awareness, then gently resume keeping track of whether you’re breathing in or out and whatever it is you are tracking. If you notice that the thoughts are wholesome, congratulate yourself for the wholesomeness of your thoughts as well. When you return to long breathing in this way, do so in a relaxed manner, not abruptly, not forcing the mind onto the breath in any way.
  • If too many thoughts, use the Ajahn Chah trick REF.
  • Never stumble into the next step. Sometimes you find yourself there naturally, without having intended to move on yet - attention drifts forward on its own. It’s better to first return to the step you’re at, and only move into the next step through intention, not by drifting onto it.

First Tetrad: Following the Body (Kāyānupassanā)

Step 1 – Long Breath Awareness

Either while breathing in long one understands ‘I breathe in long’, or while breathing out long one understands ‘I breathe out long’;

Dīghaṁ vā assasanto ’dīghaṁ assasāmī’ti pajānāti, dīghaṁ vā passasanto ’dīghaṁ passasāmī’ti pajānāti;

How to Practice
  • Continuously keep track of whether you’re breathing in or out.
  • Take intentionally long breaths, lowering the diaphragm as you inhale, to press on the gut and abdominal organs, without forcing or exaggeration. Optionally, you may make the out-breath longer than the in-breath, since modern science has confirmed that this further calms the nervous system REF.
  • Pay attention to how long breathing affects the body and mind. You may notice some ways in which long breathing, especially when done from the belly and when the inhale is longer, relaxes the body and mind.
  • Pay attention to how the state of the body and mind affects the long breathing.
Signs of Maturation
  • The body and mind feel more relaxed as a result of the deep, long breathing. Stress indicators such as blood pressure and pulse rate drop.
  • As the body relaxes, its inclination toward natural shorter breaths increases. If you notice you’ve already drifted into shorter breathing without intending to, it’s a strong indication you’re ready for Step 2. Return to long breathing briefly, then intentionally transition to short breathing.
Common misunderstandings
  • When intentionally extending the breath, it’s possible to make it too long. Stay gentle here: there is no need to force the breath to full lung capacity on the inhale, or all the way to empty on the exhale.

Step 2 – Short Breath Awareness

either while breathing in short one understands ‘I breathe in short’, or while breathing out short one understands ‘I breathe out short’;

rassaṁ vā assasanto ’rassaṁ assasāmī’ti pajānāti, rassaṁ vā passasanto ’rassaṁ passasāmī’ti pajānāti;

How to Practice
  • Continuously keep track of whether you’re breathing in or out.
  • Switch to shorter breaths without forcing or exaggeration, such that each leg of the breath is shorter in duration than what it was in Step 1. Use chest breathing rather than abdominal breathing.
  • You may shorten the breath gradually or rapidly, that’s up to you.
  • Pay attention to how short breathing affects the body and mind. You may notice some ways in which short chest breathing can increase stress or excitement or energy levels in the body and mind.
  • Pay attention to how the state of the body and mind affects the short breathing.
Signs of Maturation
  • Each breath is shorter than it was in Step 1.

Step 3 – Experiencing the Whole Body

one trains ‘I will breathe in experiencing the whole body’, one trains ‘I will breathe out experiencing the whole body’;

’sabbakāyapaṭisaṁvedī assasissāmī’ti sikkhati, ’sabbakāyapaṭisaṁvedī passasissāmī’ti sikkhati;

How to Practice
  • Continuously keep track of whether you’re breathing in or out.
  • Stop controlling breath length – let the body breathe on its own; let it automatically control breath length.
  • On each in-breath and out-breath, tune in to sensations in the body – anywhere in the body where they come up – visualizing the river of flowing sensations. Include all sensations, even pain or tension, within this field of awareness, not isolating, not focusing on any single part of the body but taking all sensations together.
  • Sense the whole body breathing. Sense the breathing throughout the body as a unified process. You may visualize oxygen energizing every cell in the body and the out-breath expelling the CO2 that cells don’t need.
  • Notice how the whole body moves with each breath. These can be subtle micro-movements or all kinds of other motions that may occur.
Signs of Maturation
  • The body is experienced as a continuous, unified presence rather than fragmented parts.
  • Discomfort, tension or pain is noticed but no longer monopolizes attention.
  • Attention remains steady across the entire body field.
Common misunderstandings
  • By this step the meditator has been sitting for a while and pain might arise more strongly as time goes by. Pain easily drives other hindrances such as sensual desire (to be pain free) or ill will (toward the pain) or restlessness (impatience for the pain to go away). If pain arrives more strongly as time goes by in this step, try to include it in the whole body stream of sensation and maintain awareness of the whole body. However, don’t let pain in step 3 prevent you from moving into step 4 — the calming of bodily formation in step 4 may help with the pain.
