Preparation

Before starting formal meditation practice, it’s essential to spend some time establishing conditions that support stability and continuity of attention. When you build a house, the stronger the foundation, the more likely it is to withstand a storm. In the same way, we build a strong foundation for each meditation session so that it is not easily toppled.

Prep 1 – Posture

We start by setting our posture. A good posture for meditation balances alertness and uprightness with ease and relaxation. Some muscular effort is always needed to keep the body aligned, but we aim to use as little muscle energy as needed to maintain balance without becoming rigid. Part of avoiding rigidity is recognizing that the body is never truly still - it is always in subtle motion, and this motion is tied to the breathing process. Seeing this clearly helps us notice the body’s natural movement rather than resist it. The goal is not perfect posture but a position that lets the breath move naturally and awareness remain steady.

Prep 2 – Loving Kindness Meditation

There are numerous methods for loving kindness meditation, and every teacher interprets these methods differently. The specific method matters less than including two categories of beings in your well-wishes: (1) yourself, and (2) all beings everywhere. See Appendix REF for a description of some loving kindness methods you could use here.

This phase does not need to be lengthy - a couple of minutes can suffice. You may silently repeat a phrase, such as “May I be safe, happy and well” a few times, followed by repeating “May all beings everywhere be safe, happy and well.” Whatever method you use, try to generate the feeling of the brahmavihara you’re practicing. Loving kindness practice softens the heart, helping to reduce hindrances like aversion and restlessness.

Prep 3 – Motivation

The reasons for pursuing formal meditation practice are as numerous as its benefits - spiritual, mental health related, or otherwise - and every person has their own. Whatever yours are, bring them to mind by silently asking yourself, “Why am I doing this?” This helps reduce the hindrance of doubt, gives the mind clearer direction from the start, and is encouraging.

Prep 4 – Session Plan

Briefly go over the plan for this formal meditation session. Check in and ask yourself how your mind is doing: is it exhilarated, agitated, relaxed, contracted, exalted, or something else? Here you can tweak the plan to fit your state of mind. It’s useful to pre-plan any changes to your usual routine, so fewer decisions have to be made during the meditation itself. For example, your plan might specify how long to spend on particular steps, or it may include a practice variation you’re trying out.

Prep 5 – Present Moment Awareness

Abandon the past. Abandon the future. Shift your attention to the present moment. If you need a tool to help with this, you can try cycling between (a) telling yourself “I am aware that,” then (b) paying attention to whatever arises - thought, touch, sound, odor, taste, or sight - and noticing how your awareness of it rises and falls.

The Sixteen Steps

Practice Instructions Common to All Steps

  • Continuously keep track of whether you’re breathing in or out.
  • Ideally, breathe both in and out through the nose. If that’s not possible or easy, breathe in through the nose and out through the mouth. As a last resort, breathe both in and out through the mouth.

First Tetrad – Following the Body – Kāyānupassanā

Step 1 – Long Breath Awareness

Either while breathing in long one understands ‘I breathe in long’, or while breathing out long one understands ‘I breathe out long’;

Dīghaṁ vā assasanto ’dīghaṁ assasāmī’ti pajānāti, dīghaṁ vā passasanto ’dīghaṁ passasāmī’ti pajānāti;

How to Practice

  • Take intentional deep, long breaths, lowering the diaphragm as you inhale, to press on the gut and abdominal organs, without forcing or exaggeration. Optionally, you may make the out-breath longer than the in-breath, since modern science has confirmed that this further calms the nervous system REF.
  • You may take just three or four such deep, long breaths, or you may continue for many minutes, depending on how much calming your mind and body require - which differs from session to session. For example, if you’re stressed or agitated when you begin, you may need to spend more time on this step, continuing until the body and mind calm down considerably.
  • If thoughts arise, and you notice them, congratulate yourself for having noticed them, for having reconnected with your awareness, and gently resume keeping track of whether you’re breathing in or out while taking long breaths. If you notice that the thoughts are wholesome, congratulate yourself for the wholesomeness of your thoughts as well. When you return to long breathing in this way, do so in a relaxed manner, not abruptly, not forcing the mind onto the breath in any way.

Signs of Maturation

  • The body and mind feel more relaxed as a result of the deep, long breathing. Stress indicators such as blood pressure and pulse rate drop.
  • As the body relaxes, its inclination toward natural shorter breaths increases. If you notice you’ve already drifted into shorter breathing without intending to, don’t treat yourself as having arrived at Step 2 (never stumble upon any step). Instead, return to long breathing briefly, then intentionally transition to short breathing.

Common misunderstandings

  • When intentionally extending the breath, it’s possible to make it too long. Stay gentle here: there is no need to force the breath to full lung capacity on the inhale, or all the way to empty on the exhale.

Step 2 – Short Breath Awareness

either while breathing in short one understands ‘I breathe in short’, or while breathing out short one understands ‘I breathe out short’;

rassaṁ vā assasanto ’rassaṁ assasāmī’ti pajānāti, rassaṁ vā passasanto ’rassaṁ passasāmī’ti pajānāti;

How to Practice

  • Switch to shorter breaths, meaning each leg of the breath takes less time, without forcing or exaggeration. Switch to whatever shorter duration feels comfortable and natural, such that when the body returns to breathing on its own, at the end of this step, it is breathing roughly at the same breath length you settle on here.
  • You may shorten the breath gradually or rapidly – whatever suis you.
  • Gradually allow the body to breathe on its own.

Signs of Maturation

  • Each breath is shorter than it was in Step 1.
  • The body and mind settle further and become more still.
  • The body is breathing on its own.
  • The breath may begin to lengthen after the body breathes on its own and if that happens, that’s a strong sign we are done with this step.

Common misunderstandings

Some meditators report the breath as actually lengthening as the body and mind become more still. In Step 1, we need to make the breath longer than the body’s natural breath. If we don’t lengthen the breath enough in Step 1, then when we let the body breathe on its own at the end of this step, it actually lengthens and does not become short.
